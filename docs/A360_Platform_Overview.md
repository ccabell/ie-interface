# **Aesthetics360 (A360) Platform Overview**

## **1\. Overall Vision and Objectives**

Aesthetics360 (A360) is positioned as an **AI-native operating system for aesthetic medicine** – a field where revenue is “created or lost in conversation” during patient consultations. The core vision is to **capture the rich data in consult conversations and transform it into structured insights** that drive clinical operations and business growth. Traditional EMRs focus on documentation but **“discard the clinical and commercial signals that drive conversion, retention, and lifetime value”**, leaving a blind spot in understanding why some consultations convert to treatments and others don’t. A360’s objective is to eliminate that blind spot by **treating the consultation transcript as the highest-signal dataset** – not a disposable note, but a source of truth for patient intent, concerns, and buying signals.

In practice, A360 **captures every patient consultation (audio and transcript) and extracts structured data on clinical details *and* commercial intent**. This data then powers a suite of tools: automated note documentation, personalized treatment/care plans, follow-up marketing outreach, performance coaching, and revenue analytics. The overarching goal is to turn conversations into actionable intelligence so that aesthetic practices can **improve conversion rates, increase per-consult revenue, and standardize best practices** across providers. By instrumenting the consultation process, A360 enables clinics to measure and influence factors like plan clarity, objection handling, and follow-up – ultimately driving higher treatment acceptance and patient lifetime value.

## **2\. Key System Components**

### **Consultation Transcription & Intelligence Extraction**

At the foundation of A360 is its ability to **capture the consultation conversation and derive structured intelligence from it**. Every patient consult is recorded and transcribed using a **domain-trained transcription service (“A360 Scribe”)** that is tuned for aesthetic medicine – preserving important nuances like patient goals, objections, pricing questions, and urgency signals in addition to clinical content. Rather than ending up with just raw text, the transcript is run through A360’s **Intelligence Extraction Engine**, a pipeline of AI agents (built on AWS Bedrock LLM models) that produces an evidence-backed profile of the consult. This engine operates under strict guardrails to avoid hallucination: **it only outputs facts or fields that can be explicitly supported by transcript evidence** (with direct citation of the speaker’s words).

The extracted intelligence is quite granular. The system identifies **“atomic facts”** from the dialogue (e.g. specific patient concerns, stated goals, treatments mentioned), each tagged with who said it, the exact quote, timestamp, and confidence. It also generates higher-level structured outputs, including a JSON summary of **“Patient Intelligence”** (key findings like summary of concerns, metrics, products/services discussed, next steps, opportunities) and a set of **commercial signals/KPIs** for that consult. Importantly, A360 grounds any treatment or product mentioned against the practice’s **offer catalog** – mapping to known service IDs if possible, so that recommendations align with what the practice actually offers (and flagging anything that doesn’t match the catalog).

In real-time, A360 can even perform lightweight **live signal tagging** – for example, flagging a major objection or intent moment as the consult is happening – but the authoritative extraction occurs **post-consult in a single pass** to write official data to the record. The net result is that **every consultation yields a structured consult profile**: a combination of clinical details and commercial intent signals stored for that patient. This transforms the transient conversation into durable data that can be queried and acted upon. As the team puts it, **“A360’s core insight is that the consultation transcript is not disposable” – it is the richest data source for understanding patient needs and purchase intent**.

### **KPI and Coaching Frameworks**

One of A360’s differentiating features is the creation of **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) and coaching feedback** from consultations. Because A360 captures not just what was discussed but how it was handled, it can measure provider performance on critical consult behaviors. For example, the system tracks metrics like **whether objections were resolved**, if pricing was discussed appropriately, if a clear summary and next steps were provided, etc. Each consultation populates a **“consultation commercial signals” KPI record in Postgres** – essentially a row of metrics derived from that conversation. These KPIs roll up into provider and practice dashboards, allowing leadership to spot patterns (e.g. one provider has a high rate of unresolved objections or low plan clarity). The **Intelligence \+ Coach** module then closes the loop by providing **automated coaching insights**: it compares what happened in the consult to best-practice standards and offers feedback or training tips.

The coaching framework behind this is built on a library of **consultation best practices and playbooks**. A360 has defined a **5-phase “Consultation Excellence” framework** (rapport building, discovery, education, planning, and closing) and uses it as a lens to evaluate each consult. In addition, a series of deep-dive playbooks target specific skills: **Objection Handling**, **Closing techniques**, **Educating to build trust**, **Financial discussion skills**, **Follow-up and re-engagement**, etc.. The system references these playbooks in its prompts to the AI evaluator, so it can score the consultation on each dimension and provide targeted feedback. For instance, if a patient raised a concern about pain and the provider brushed it off, the coaching output might flag this and remind the provider to use the “LAER” framework – **Listen, Acknowledge, Explore, Respond** – for handling objections. The **Coaching Output** after each consult thus highlights what went well and what could be improved, often accompanied by example quotes from the transcript and references to best practices (e.g. praising a provider for using empathetic language, or suggesting they should have summarized the plan at the end).

The KPIs provide a quantitative backbone for this coaching. Each metric is clearly defined and benchmarked. For example, **“Consult Ended Without Summary”** is a KPI that tracks if the provider failed to recap the visit. If a practice ends 10% of consults with no summary, that might be around industry average (10–15%), whereas high performers are below 5%. In one real case, a practice had only 1 out of 76 consults without a summary (\~1.3%), rated as **“Excellent” (better than high performers)**. Conversely, **“Unresolved Objections”** is a critical KPI – A360 found one practice left 64% of objections unaddressed (vs. a 10–15% rate for high performers). The system flagged this as a major issue impacting conversion, and the coaching output for that practice included urgent recommendations (e.g. **train staff on proper objection handling using LAER, develop scripts for common objections, set a target to reduce unresolved objections below 15%**). In summary, A360 converts subjective consult interactions into objective metrics and coaching cues, creating a continuous improvement loop to boost consult effectiveness and, ultimately, revenue conversion.

### **Opportunity Extraction and Tracking**

A key commercial outcome of any consult is the set of **treatment opportunities** – services or products the patient is interested in or could be persuaded to adopt. A360 automates the identification and tracking of these opportunities. Using the transcript, the system performs **“Opportunity Extraction”** to catalog all treatments or products that were discussed (even indirectly) and note their status (e.g. was it recommended by the provider? Did the patient express strong interest or hesitance? Was it booked or deferred?). This provides a structured view of potential revenue opportunities from the consult. The framework classifies opportunities into categories such as: **Primary opportunity** (the main treatment of focus or the patient’s chief goal), **Secondary opportunities** (additional or upsell treatments discussed), **Future opportunities** (deferred for later), and even **Missed opportunities** (services that could fit the patient’s needs but weren’t brought up). For each, the system captures details like the specific service (e.g. Botox, laser resurfacing), area (if applicable), whether pricing was discussed, patient’s intent level (high/medium/low), and the next step (scheduled, thinking about it, etc.), all backed by transcript quotes as evidence.

Crucially, A360’s pipeline first determines the **consultation type** (initial visit, follow-up, treatment day, pricing inquiry, or mixed) to properly interpret the context. The consult type influences what counts as an opportunity. For example, in a follow-up or treatment visit, the **“primary opportunity”** might be the treatment the patient came in for that day (since the decision to treat is essentially already made), whereas upsell opportunities might be ancillary services. In an initial consult, the primary opportunity is whatever core treatment plan was discussed/recommended, and secondary opportunities are additional suggestions the provider made. A360’s **visit type detection** looks for linguistic signals in the transcript (e.g. “first time here” → initial consult, or “last time we did…” → follow-up) to classify the encounter. This classification is then used to tailor the opportunity extraction logic and output relevant fields for that scenario.

Once extracted, these opportunities are **tracked for follow-through**. A360 can push them into an internal opportunity management view or sync with external CRM systems. In fact, the platform integrates with GoHighLevel (a CRM/marketing platform) and others to create deals or tasks for each consult opportunity so that no revenue opportunity slips through the cracks. Each opportunity can be assigned a conversion probability or value (some of these calculations exist in V1 and are being refined in V2). For example, a patient who said “I’ll think about CoolSculpting” might be logged as an open opportunity with a certain estimated value and a follow-up task in the pipeline. A360’s forthcoming **Opportunity Tracking V2** will deepen this capability – introducing **configurable probability weights, value multipliers for each stage, and time-decay for aging opportunities**, along with a more robust integration to CRM pipelines. Already, the system maps A360’s opportunity statuses to CRM stages (e.g. marking an opportunity as “hot”, “deferred”, “booked”, or “lost” and reflecting that in GHL’s pipeline). By extracting opportunities automatically and tracking them, A360 ensures that when a patient doesn’t book on the spot, the potential revenue is not lost – it’s entered into a follow-up workflow.

### **Email Follow-Up and Automation (A360 Reach)**

**A360 Reach** is the platform’s automated follow-up and marketing engine that “closes the loop” after the consultation. Its purpose is to turn the consult insights into **hyper-personalized outreach** that nurtures the patient toward booking. Reach connects A360’s intelligence to external marketing systems like GoHighLevel (GHL). As soon as the consult intelligence is available, Reach can push **custom tags/fields** into GHL or a similar system via API, triggering tailored email or SMS campaigns. The unique value here is that follow-ups are not generic drip emails – they are **consult-aware and context-driven**. For example, if during the consult the patient mentioned, “I have a wedding in August and I’m interested in Botox,” A360 will extract those facts (wedding timing \+ interest in Botox). **Reach can then automatically schedule a targeted campaign** – perhaps an email in June saying “Your big day is coming up – here’s how we can help you glow with a Botox treatment plan in time for August”. This kind of precisely-timed, context-specific follow-up dramatically increases patient engagement compared to one-size-fits-all marketing.

Under the hood, **Reach uses “intent-based triggers.”** It converts transcript-derived data (expressed interests, stated timelines or life events, motivations) into structured triggers that match marketing campaigns. A patient’s profile in GHL might get tagged with “Interest: LaserResurfacing” or “Event: Wedding\_08/2026” and automatically enter the appropriate campaign sequence. A360 has a defined schema for what it sends – e.g. contact info plus custom fields like intent tags, timing, and recommended campaign – all via JSON webhook payloads to the marketing platform. On the flip side, Reach can also ingest data back (via inbound webhooks) on campaign performance – such as whether the patient opened emails or clicked – and surface that to the practice.

From a user perspective, A360 Reach provides an **internal dashboard** for providers and practice managers to see the status of these automations. The dashboard shows which campaigns have been triggered for each patient, and how those campaigns are performing (open rates, click-through, etc.). This gives the practice visibility and control – they can monitor re-engagement and even intervene (e.g. if a patient responds or books, the campaign can be stopped). While the initial focus of Reach is email/SMS follow-up, the roadmap extends to broader **multi-channel engagement** (social media, etc.) and a full feedback loop into patient reactivation and retention. The ultimate objective is to **“drive higher conversions through intelligent, timely outreach,”** essentially using A360’s AI to ensure every lead is followed up with the right message at the right time. Reach turns the consult conversation into a personalized marketing touchpoint, boosting conversion and revenue without the staff having to manually manage follow-ups.

### **Treatment and Care Plan Generation (TCP Engine)**

The **Treatment & Care Plan (TCP)** engine in A360 automatically generates a personalized care plan document for the patient based on the consultation. This addresses the crucial handoff after a consult: providing the patient with a clear, professional plan that summarizes recommendations, timeline, and next steps. The **“Plan”** product in A360 “**transforms the consult into a patient-ready treatment plan and sales asset**,” effectively turning the live conversation into a tangible plan of care. This serves multiple purposes: it ensures the patient understands the recommendations (improving conversion and trust), creates a record of what was proposed, and gives the practice a consistent way to present treatments (which also helps identify missed opportunities or objections from the plan data).

The TCP engine works by taking the **structured consult intelligence** (especially the patient’s goals, concerns, and any treatments discussed or recommended) and using it to compose a detailed plan. Behind the scenes, it follows a multi-step AI workflow. In **Step 1**, it pulls out key inputs from the transcript: the patient’s stated **concerns and goals**, any **treatments or products mentioned** (or implied needs), and the patient’s **emotional tone/readiness**. It cross-references the practice’s **product & service library** to attach relevant details to those treatments (e.g. if “chemical peel” was discussed, the system knows from the library what a standard protocol for that is, pricing range, aftercare instructions, etc.). In the current implementation this library lookup is facilitated by a RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) approach – using a **temporary practice products/services library** as context for the AI. (A future improvement is to fully integrate the practice’s official catalog into the database and pipeline).

In **Step 2**, the AI uses these insights to **draft a structured Treatment & Care Plan** in JSON form. The plan is typically organized by the patient’s concerns or goals. For each concern, the AI will suggest one or more **recommended treatments**, including details like frequency or number of sessions (if known), and it automatically pulls in any **post-treatment care instructions** associated with those treatments. It can also list relevant **skincare products** if those are part of the comprehensive plan (e.g. recommending a post-procedure skincare regimen). The draft plan remains cautious: it will mark items that are uncertain or need provider input (for example, if it’s unsure whether to choose Botox vs. Dysport for a given patient, it may include a placeholder like “Neurotoxin treatment – *Provider to confirm choice*” with a flag that provider selection is required). The AI also attaches placeholders for anything it can’t determine (like pricing, if not configured) so that nothing is assumed without validation.

In **Step 3**, A360 provides a **human-in-the-loop interface for the provider to review and customize the plan**. Through a web dashboard, the provider can see the AI-generated recommendations and make edits: accept or remove certain suggestions, tweak the number of sessions or the specific product brand, add any treatments that were discussed but maybe didn’t get captured, and so forth. The system is designed to be flexible to each practice’s workflow – for example, the practice can configure which fields appear on the final plan (some might want to include pricing estimates, others might prefer to discuss price separately; some might include detailed post-care instructions, others might keep it high-level). The provider’s edits are saved, and then in **Step 4** the system can run a **validation check against practice SOPs** – e.g. ensuring the plan complies with any rules (if the practice has rules about sequencing treatments or contraindications). Finally, in **Step 5**, the plan is finalized into a patient-facing format (like a nicely formatted PDF or digital document) which can be shared with the patient, and key data points from the plan (selected treatments, timeline, etc.) can be pushed to the CRM or A360 Reach for follow-up automation.

The TCP is where **clinical recommendations meet sales and marketing**. It not only lists treatments but also follows best practices to encourage adherence and cross-sell. The design goals for the plan include: phasing treatments over time (to increase adherence and return visits), suggesting complementary services for a comprehensive plan (cross-sell to boost value), and presenting it in a **“visually clear, emotionally compelling”** way that resonates with patients. In essence, A360’s treatment plan engine ensures every patient gets a personalized, professional care plan at the end of the consult, which drives home the value of the consultation and sets the stage for higher conversion and long-term engagement.

### **Patient Intelligence Dashboard**

All the data extracted from the consultation is ultimately presented back to the users (providers and staff) through an intuitive UI – often referred to as the **Patient Intelligence Dashboard or Tab**. This is a part of the A360 web application where, for each consultation (and patient), the team can review the key insights mined by the AI. The **Patient Intelligence tab** is typically organized into UI-ready blocks such as: **Summary** of the consultation, key **Metrics** (like sentiment or intent scores), **Products/Services discussed**, **Next Steps** decided, and **Opportunities** identified. In other words, it’s a one-stop overview of what happened in the consult and what to do next, all generated automatically. A provider can click into a patient’s record and immediately see, for example: *“Summary: Patient is concerned about fine lines, interested in Botox and laser; Metrics: Patient sentiment was very positive, plan clarity 90%; Products/Services: Botox (discussed), Retinol cream (recommended); Next Steps: follow-up call in 1 week; Opportunities: consider chemical peel in future.”* This saves time and ensures no detail is missed when handing off between staff or doing follow-ups.

Because the Patient Intelligence dashboard is populated directly from the evidence-backed extraction, it increases confidence in the data. Providers can even drill down to see the exact transcript snippet behind a particular data point if needed. For example, if an “Opportunity” listed is *“Laser Hair Removal (patient to consider in winter)”*, the staff could trace that back to the patient’s comment, say, “Maybe I’ll do laser hair removal after summer.” This transparency builds trust in the AI suggestions.

In addition to patient-specific intelligence, A360 also offers **aggregate dashboards for practice intelligence and coaching**. These might be considered part of the broader “Coaching & Business Intelligence” module. Leadership can view KPIs across all consultations – for instance, seeing each provider’s conversion rate, objection resolution rate, average revenue per consult, etc., in a dashboard format. This is accompanied by tools to dive into specific consults or play call recordings for qualitative review. There is also an **internal “Opportunity Board” or CRM view** that lists all outstanding opportunities extracted (e.g. all patients who said they were interested but haven’t booked yet), along with their status and value. This allows the practice’s sales coordinator or manager to proactively manage the sales pipeline post-consult. (Opportunities can be sorted by value or likelihood, and the staff can ensure each one gets followed up via Reach or personal outreach.) In essence, the Patient Intelligence dashboard and related UI components turn the flood of consult data into actionable, digestible information for both front-line providers and managers, enhancing both patient care and business performance.

## **3\. Architecture and Technical Stack**

### **Core Databases and Data Storage**

A360’s platform uses a mix of structured and unstructured data storage, optimized for both **strict accuracy and flexibility**. The **system-of-record database is Aurora PostgreSQL**, which stores all the core entities: practices, providers, patients, consultations, etc., as well as the outputs of the AI extraction. Each consultation has a row in a `consultations` table (with basic info like date, participants, etc.), and there are related tables for the **transcript** (often storing the raw text or a reference to S3 where audio/text is stored), the **extracted intelligence facts**, and the **KPI metrics**. The design follows an **append-only, auditable approach** for AI-generated data: rather than overwriting any patient info with AI guesses, A360 stores fine-grained “facts” with evidence. For example, if a patient said they have a wedding in August, an atomic fact like `event_wedding_date=August` is stored with a pointer to the transcript line. This means the database accumulates a detailed profile over time that is traceable to source. Derived tables then aggregate these facts – for instance, a **patient\_profile table** might hold the latest key facts about the patient (goals, top concerns, etc.), and a **consultation\_commercial\_kpis table** stores one row per consult with metrics (like `num_objections`, `conversion_outcome`, etc.) for reporting.

One notable design choice is the use of **JSON vs. structured columns**. The AI’s output initially is a complex JSON (with nested evidence, lists of opportunities, etc.), and in early iterations the team considered simply storing that JSON as-is. However, to enable easy querying and joining (for dashboards and integration), they parse most fields into structured Postgres columns. The schema is carefully designed and even included a DDL script for the consultation intelligence tables. For example, they might have a table for `consultation_opportunities` with columns like `service`, `status`, `patient_intent_level`, `estimated_value`, etc., rather than one big JSON blob – this allows writing SQL to find all “hot” opportunities or to compute conversion rates. That said, A360 still stores the raw JSON output or transcripts in places where fidelity is needed. **Transcripts** are typically stored in full (possibly in an S3 bucket with a reference in Postgres) to preserve every word. **Evidence payloads** might be kept as JSON or in a separate evidence table linked by foreign keys. The system also incorporates a **vector index** (likely using a tool like OpenSearch or an embedding database) for unstructured queries – e.g. enabling a future feature where a user could search all transcripts for certain phrases or do semantic searches. Indeed, the documentation suggests a hybrid approach: **“Structured queries (Postgres) \+ unstructured retrieval (vector index)”**, acknowledging both are useful.

Beyond Postgres, A360 uses other storage as needed. **Amazon S3** is used for large payloads and logs – e.g. storing the original audio files, the raw transcript files, and intermediate JSON outputs from the AI (especially in early prototypes). In the initial versions of the coaching and opportunity extraction, the results were simply dumped as JSON files in S3 for simplicity. As the system matured, more of this moved into the database proper. **DynamoDB** is also utilized in specific roles; one mention is using Dynamo as a job tracking store or for fast lookup of real-time tags during the live transcription phase. For example, as segments of the transcript stream in, quick real-time classifications might be cached in Dynamo for immediate use (like highlighting an objection on the UI live).

A360 exposes its data and functionality through a set of **APIs (likely REST or GraphQL)** that the frontend and integration partners consume. The core application backend is developed in a modern web framework (the tasks mention possibly FastAPI/Flask in context), and it provides endpoints for retrieving the patient intelligence, dashboards, etc. For instance, when the frontend needs to display the cards for a processed consult, it calls an API endpoint (e.g. `GET /consultations/{id}/intelligence`) which pulls from Postgres the summary, KPIs, and opportunities to return as JSON. There are also endpoints to trigger processing – e.g., when a transcript is finalized, an endpoint can be hit to initiate the extraction process (if not event-driven). These APIs ensure that the UI and any external systems can seamlessly get the insights stored in the A360 platform.

Security and compliance are carefully handled: all PHI (Protected Health Information) is encrypted at rest in the databases, and access is governed by strict IAM roles and scoped by practice (multi-tenant considerations). Given the sensitivity of consult data, A360 has likely implemented role-based access, audit logging, and will consider features like row-level security as the product scales. In summary, the tech stack’s backbone is **AWS \+ Postgres**, with a balance between JSON for flexibility and structured schema for reliability. This allows A360 to be both **high-integrity (healthcare-grade data correctness)** and **highly agile in developing new AI-driven features**.

### **AWS Infrastructure and AI Orchestration**

A360’s platform is built on AWS and leverages a serverless-oriented architecture to handle the AI-driven workflows. At a high level, the data flow for post-consult processing looks like this: **Consultation audio is streamed and saved → Transcript stored in S3 and/or DB → An event triggers an AWS Step Functions state machine → The state machine orchestrates a series of Lambda functions (which call Bedrock for AI) → Outputs are saved to the database and S3**. Each piece of this corresponds to AWS services:

* **Data Ingestion and Trigger**: As the consultation happens, audio is streamed (the system uses WebSockets and likely Amazon Kinesis for real-time streaming). The audio is transcribed either via a specialized model or potentially AWS Transcribe if configured, and once a transcript is finalized, an event is emitted. A360 uses Amazon **EventBridge** (or possibly an SQS message) to catch the "transcript finalized" event and trigger the next stage. This decouples the recording process from the AI extraction process.

* **Step Functions Orchestration**: The heavy lifting of the intelligence extraction is coordinated by an AWS **Step Functions** state machine. The team initially experimented with doing everything in one Bedrock workflow, but for flexibility and reliability they chose an explicit Step Functions flow. The state machine breaks the task into modular steps, each executed by a Lambda. For instance, steps might include: *Validate Input* (check transcript exists, load metadata), *Extract Visit Type* (determine consultation type via a Bedrock call), *Get Practice Catalog* (retrieve the practice’s service/product catalog from the database), then parallel branches to *Extract Primary Opportunity*, *Extract Secondary Opportunities*, *Extract Future Opportunities* (each could be a Lambda calling Bedrock with prompts focused on those categories). After parallel extraction, it goes to *Validate/Enrich* (e.g., a Lambda to validate that extracted services actually exist in the catalog and enrich them with IDs), then *Calculate Metrics* (compute any summary stats like total opportunities or conversion likelihood), and finally *Store Results* (write everything to the database). This structured approach gives A360 fine-grained control: each step can have retry logic, error handling, and timeouts defined in the Step Functions definition. For example, if a Bedrock call fails or times out, that state can retry or fall back to a simpler approach. The design also allows parallelization – as noted, extracting different opportunity types can happen in parallel branches, which speeds up processing.

* **Bedrock and AI Models**: A360 uses **Amazon Bedrock** to access large language models (LLMs) like Anthropic’s Claude. The prompts and schemas for the extraction agents are carefully designed (there are different prompt templates for real-time tagging, for the final extraction, for JSON validation/repair, etc.). In V1, the team chose a two-model strategy: a faster, lower-cost model (Claude “Haiku” presumably) for quick real-time tasks, and a more powerful model (Claude “Sonnet”) for the final full extraction. The Step Functions pipeline calls Bedrock via AWS Lambda functions – essentially each Lambda forms the request to Bedrock’s API with the appropriate model ID and prompt, and gets the response. Special care is taken to validate the AI output: after getting JSON from the model, A360 runs it through a **JSON schema validator**. If it’s invalid or missing required fields, there is even a “JSON repair” step (another Bedrock call with a prompt to fix formatting). Nothing is written to the database unless it passes all validations (including that evidence is provided for each field). This stringent approach (with multi-step Bedrock agents and verification) is designed to ensure **“nothing becomes truth unless backed by transcript evidence”**.

* **Event-Driven Updates**: After the extraction pipeline completes and data is stored, A360 emits events or triggers to update the UI and other services. For example, it might dispatch an internal event like `consult_intelligence.ready` which the web app listens for to know it can refresh the dashboard. Also, if integration with external systems (like CRM) is enabled, separate processes or Lambdas handle those. A360’s integration with GHL uses a service that, upon new opportunity data, will call the GHL API to create/update an Opportunity there. This syncing logic is carefully managed to avoid duplicates or excessive calls (e.g., not syncing an opportunity more than once within a 5-minute window unless forced).

Overall, the architecture is cloud-native and scalable. Using Step Functions and Lambdas means it can handle many consults in parallel, and scale down to zero when not in use (cost-efficient). The design also isolates any heavy processing in asynchronous flows so that the end-user app remains responsive (the provider can continue charting or talking while the analysis runs in the background). Moreover, by using AWS services like Kinesis, EventBridge, Lambda, and Bedrock, the team offloads a lot of the undifferentiated heavy lifting (scaling, security, model hosting) to AWS – letting them focus on the domain-specific logic.

For front-end and other components: the A360 web application is likely a React/Next.js frontend (as hinted by the Next.js mention in docs), communicating with the backend over secure APIs. The **UI updates in near-real-time** after a consult; e.g., once the intelligence is ready, the cards appear without the need for a full page refresh (they probably use websockets or polling to get the results, given the emphasis on immediate display).

Finally, A360 also integrates with external systems through a well-defined interface. They use **OAuth2 or API keys** for authentication when connecting to practice CRM/EMR systems. For example, to integrate with an EMR like Zenoti, A360 might call Zenoti’s APIs to fetch a practice’s service list (populating the product library) or to push notes and plans back. The executive brief notes that A360 has already integrated with **Zenoti (an EMR) and GoHighLevel**, and is adding more integrations via partners like Kareo/Karegon. This integration strategy means A360 can sit on top of existing practice management software as an intelligence layer, rather than requiring clinics to rip-and-replace their whole system.

### **Data Flow from Transcript to Intelligence**

Bringing it all together: the **lifecycle of data** in A360 starts with a conversation and ends with actionable outputs:

1. **Capture** – The patient and provider have a consultation (in-person or virtual). A360’s app captures the audio; the conversation is transcribed (with domain-specific accuracy). The raw transcript (segmented by speaker and timestamped) is saved as the authoritative record of the consult.

2. **Extraction Pipeline** – Once the consult ends, A360 triggers the AI extraction flow. The transcript (plus relevant context like which practice and which provider, and that practice’s service catalog) is fed into a series of AI prompts. The output is a structured JSON containing: identified facts with evidence, a set of consultation KPI values, a Patient Intelligence summary (for the dashboard), a list of opportunities, and even a preliminary consult revenue “plan” (if applicable). This is processed and validated as described, then important pieces are written into Postgres tables.

3. **Activation of Data** – Immediately, multiple things happen with the extracted intelligence:

   * **Documentation**: The provider gets a draft clinical note or summary of the consult (for example, A360 can generate the consult note for the EMR based on the transcript intelligence, saving the doctor documentation time).

   * **Treatment Plan**: The Treatment Plan engine (if enabled for that consult type) produces a patient-facing care plan document using the extracted goals and recommendations, which the provider reviews and shares with the patient.

   * **Dashboard Update**: The Patient Intelligence dashboard in the app populates with the new consult’s data – the provider can see the summary and metrics, and a manager viewing the coaching dashboard will see that consult’s KPIs roll into their reports.

   * **Follow-up Actions**: Any **Opportunities** not closed are either sent to an internal opportunity board or synced to the CRM for the sales team. Simultaneously, **A360 Reach** triggers any needed automated follow-ups (emails/SMS) based on the consult (using the specific intents and timing extracted).

   * **Storage**: The transcript and all intelligence are stored for long-term analysis. This growing dataset (hundreds, then thousands of consults) becomes a goldmine for benchmarking. Over time A360 can analyze across all this data to refine its models (e.g., learning what language correlates with conversion) and provide industry benchmarks to clients (e.g., “your average consult-to-treatment conversion is 60% vs an industry avg of 55%”).

In sum, A360’s data flow ensures that **the conversation turns into structured data within minutes, and that data in turn drives multiple outcomes** – note generation, patient plan, CRM tasks, follow-up campaigns, and performance analytics. By the next day (or even immediately after the visit), the practice has both the outputs for the patient (notes, plan, follow-up emails scheduled) and the internal insights for management (KPI dashboards updated), all derived from one source – the conversation itself. This seamless flow from unstructured human dialogue to structured, multi-faceted output is the heart of A360’s technical achievement.

## **4\. UI and UX Considerations**

### **Patient-Facing vs. Provider-Facing Interfaces**

A360 consciously designs outputs for both the patient and the provider, recognizing they have different needs. On the **patient-facing** side, the primary element is the **Treatment/Care Plan**. This is presented as a clean, easy-to-understand document (often a PDF or printed handout) that the patient can take with them. It’s written in patient-friendly language, summarizing the recommended treatments, the timeline or phases (e.g. what to do now vs later), and any product recommendations or at-home care instructions. The plan serves as both a medical and sales document – it reinforces the value of the proposed treatments (educating the patient on why they’re recommended and how they address the patient’s goals) and provides a sense of personalized care. By making the plan **“visually clear \[and\] emotionally compelling”**, A360 aims to improve the patient’s confidence and acceptance of the treatment plan. The UI/UX for this output is often generated as a PDF with the practice’s branding, possibly including before/after image placeholders or testimonial snippets to further engage the patient (those features are likely on the roadmap as imaging and visualization add-ons). Patients may receive this via email or via a patient portal if the practice uses one, or simply as a printout during checkout.

Another patient-facing element is the **automated follow-up communication** (A360 Reach emails/texts). While not a “UI” in the traditional sense, crafting these communications is a UX consideration: the content needs to feel personal and relevant. A360 likely provides templates that insert dynamic fields (like “Hi \[Name\], since you mentioned \[concern\], here’s an article about it…” or “we wanted to check in about your interest in \[Treatment\]”). The style and timing of these messages are tuned carefully – e.g., a friendly check-in a week after an undecided consult, or a reminder about a pre-booked treatment. From the patient’s perspective, these are part of their experience with the clinic, so A360 ensures they are context-aware (no generic spam) and appropriately spaced. In the future, if social and multi-channel are added, the same intelligence might drive what content the patient sees on Facebook ads or other touchpoints, aligning marketing UX with the consult conversation.

On the **provider-facing** side, A360 is delivered as a web application (and possibly mobile-friendly web app) that the clinic staff and providers use daily. The UI includes:

* **Consultation Workspace**: During the consult, providers might interact with A360’s interface minimally (since it’s capturing in the background). Some providers use A360 as a live scribe (seeing the transcript scroll in real-time on an iPad, for example). The interface likely allows quick corrections or flags (since providers might want to ensure medical accuracy in notes). After the consult, the provider can open the consultation record in A360 and see the outputs: the draft note, the treatment plan, and the intelligence summary. They can make edits (e.g. edit the note or adjust the plan) through a form-based UI that is simpler than writing from scratch.

* **Patient Intelligence Dashboard**: As described, this is a tab or section in the patient’s profile showing the analysis of the latest consult (or aggregate of multiple consults). The UX is tuned to be **scannable and actionable** – key info at a glance, with the ability to click for details. For example, a provider might see a highlight saying “👎 Unresolved Objection: patient had cost concern not addressed” with a red icon, prompting them to possibly follow up on that or be aware of it in the next conversation. Opportunities might be displayed with clear labels like “Potential: Laser Hair Removal – patient interested, not booked” so the front desk or coordinator can proactively mention it next time.

* **Provider KPI Dashboards**: There is likely an admin or manager view where you can compare providers on various metrics (conversion rates, avg revenue, etc.) and see trends over time. The UX here would be charts and tables, with filtering by date, provider, location, etc. For coaching, there might be a module where a regional manager can review a specific consult’s transcript alongside the AI’s evaluation and add their own notes or tags for coaching. Since the question focuses on UI, we note that these dashboards present complex data in digestible formats (using traffic light coloring for performance vs benchmarks, etc., as seen in the KPI examples where performance level is labeled “✅ Excellent” or similar).

Crucially, A360 integrates into existing provider workflows as much as possible. The **notes** that A360 generates can be viewed and edited within A360, but also exported to the clinic’s EMR if needed (since A360 isn’t trying to replace the EMR for record-keeping). The UI likely has an approval step for notes (the provider signs off that the note is correct) to meet compliance.

### **Internal Admin Tools & Opportunity Boards**

On the administrative side, A360 provides tools for practice managers and coordinators to oversee the consult-driven sales pipeline. One such tool is the **Opportunity Board**. This is conceptually like a sales CRM view, but focused on consult outcomes. Every opportunity extracted (for example, “Patient Alice – interested in CoolSculpting – follow-up scheduled”) can appear as a card or row on a board. The board might be organized by stage (Interested, Follow-up Scheduled, Consultation Booked, Treatment Booked, or Lost). As A360 syncs with CRM, this could mirror stages in GHL or another CRM, but having it in A360’s UI is useful for clinics that want to manage it in one place. The **opportunity board UI** would highlight which opportunities need action: e.g., “5 days since consult – no booking yet – consider a reach-out”. It likely shows the estimated value of each opportunity (perhaps drawn from an average treatment price or specific price if known), so the practice can prioritize high-value leads. In V1, opportunity tracking might be rudimentary (perhaps a simple table). The roadmap suggests improving this with **probability scoring and configurable thresholds in V2**, which implies the UI will gain features like sorting by conversion probability or filters (e.g., show me all “hot” opportunities).

Another admin tool is the **Reach campaign manager** (part of A360 Reach’s dashboard). Practice managers can log in and see all the automated campaigns that have gone out or are queued. The UI might list patients with their corresponding triggered campaign (e.g., “Jane Doe – Wedding Offer Email – scheduled for June 1”). It would also show engagement stats. The design likely uses charts or simple metrics (like “Emails Sent: 50, Open Rate: 60%, Click Rate: 20%” for a campaign). For example, they could maintain the **practice’s service catalog** in A360 (list of treatments, products, prices) if it’s not synced from elsewhere. A product library interface would allow them to add new services or mark ones as inactive. There may be an **“A360 Admin” interface** (used by the A360 team or advanced users) for things like monitoring the AI processing jobs, reviewing error logs, or manually re-running an extraction if needed (for example, if the AI improvements roll out, they might want to re-run old transcripts through the new model to update the intelligence). These kinds of tools ensure the system can be managed and tuned without full developer intervention.

### **V1 Card System Design**

To present the consult results in a user-friendly way, A360 introduced a **“Card System” UI in V1** for the post-consult display. Instead of dumping a wall of text or a complex report, the key information is broken into **cards** that the user can expand for details. According to the specs, there are three core card types:

1. **Consultation Summary Card** – a high-level narrative overview of the consult.

2. **KPI Cards** – each showing a specific performance metric from the consult (with a percentage score and explanation).

3. **Detailed Insight Cards (Summary with References)** – which cover specific topics like “Products and Services Discussed,” “Patient Interests,” “Next Steps,” etc., giving both a short summary and a list of items or values extracted.

The **Summary Card** provides 2-3 sentence recap of the whole consult (collapsed view) and can expand to a slightly longer version (4-6 sentences). It avoids technical jargon, simply highlighting main points (e.g. “Discussed treatment options for facial rejuvenation; patient interested in Botox and chemical peel, concerned about downtime; agreed to follow-up after vacation.”). This gives the provider a quick refresher or helps other staff quickly grasp what the consult was about.

Each **KPI Card** focuses on one key metric – the initial set in V1 includes **Patient Sentiment, Protocol Adherence, Plan Clarity, and Patient Alignment**. The card shows a percentage score (0–100%) for that metric and is color-coded or annotated if needed (green for good, etc.). For example, “Patient Sentiment: 85% 😊”. When expanded, it shows a brief analysis of that metric specifically for this consult: a one-line summary and a few bullet points of what contributed to that score. Importantly, these bullets are phrased generally (no direct quotes, just observations like “Patient asked many questions, indicating strong engagement” or “Provider addressed patient’s fears, improving sentiment”). There’s also a “?” icon that the user can click to get an **info tooltip/modal explaining how that KPI is defined and calculated** in general. For instance, clicking “Patient Sentiment ?” might say “*Sentiment measures the patient’s overall emotional tone and engagement… Higher scores mean more positive and interested.*”. This helps users trust and understand the AI scores.

The **“Summary with References” Cards** dive into specific content extracted. The prime example is **“Products and Services Discussed”**. This card would list a short summary (e.g. “Discussed several skincare and injectable options, focusing on Botox for forehead lines.”) and when expanded, it shows a list of items: *Botox – 92% confidence (discussed)*, *Retinol Cream – 85% confidence (recommended)*, *Laser Resurfacing – 75% (deferred consideration)*, etc., possibly grouped into categories like “Discussed” vs “Potential Future”. The card might include a brief quote or context for each item, but in V1 the focus is on capturing names and confidence scores. Other similar cards might include **“Patient Interests”** (if the patient explicitly mentioned goals or interests beyond treatments – e.g. “wants natural-looking results”), **“Next Steps”** (which would summarize any agreed follow-up or homework, like “Patient will consult with spouse; follow-up call scheduled”), or **“Treatment Areas”** (if relevant, highlighting what areas of the body/face were discussed for treatment).

The V1 card system was designed to **“display everything – no visit type filtering”**. This means the system errs on the side of showing all possible info extracted, rather than hiding things based on context. For example, even if it was a quick treatment visit, if the patient mentioned a new concern, the summary card will include it and the opportunities card will list it. The rationale is to **not miss any value**: if the AI found something, show it to the user, because it might be useful even if it seems out of scope for that visit. The UI is structured to handle varying amounts of information gracefully (cards just won’t have much content if a section wasn’t applicable).

The cards have a **consistent look and feel** (rounded corners, subtle shadows, as per modern dashboard design) and work responsively (stacking on mobile). Users can expand multiple cards at once (though an optional design is to auto-collapse others when one expands). The UX emphasis is on **quick readability and interactivity** – the provider can glance at the closed cards to see an outline of the consult, and then click on the ones they care about to drill in. Smooth animations make the expansion feel polished.

One can think of the card system as A360’s way of summarizing a lot of complex analysis in a **dashboard-like snippet format** right after each consult. It’s an evolving UI: future versions plan to incorporate more differentiation (for example, distinguishing in the product card which items were just mentioned in passing vs which were seriously considered – a feature slated for a later phase). Additionally, more card types could be added (like an “Objections” card listing any objections raised and how they were handled, or a “Consult Type” card that labels the encounter).

In V1 though, the three types (Summary, KPI, and Detailed) covered the main needs. The choice of four KPI cards reflected the initial metrics the team felt confident in scoring objectively across all consults. As A360’s intelligence extraction expands (e.g., measuring things like “Did provider mention pricing proactively or not”), new KPI cards might appear. The card system provides a flexible container to present new insights without overhauling the UI – each new insight can be a new card.

In summary, the A360 UI philosophy is to **surface AI-generated insights in a clear, friendly manner that integrates into the clinical workflow**. It gives immediate value after each consult (through the card dashboard and plan documents), while also feeding longer-term value (through coaching dashboards and opportunity tracking). Both patients and providers get tailored outputs that enhance their experience – patients feel heard and guided, providers get analytical backup and automation for their next steps.

## **5\. Playbooks and Best Practices**

A360’s intelligence isn’t just based on machine learning in a vacuum – it’s guided heavily by domain expertise captured in **playbooks and best-practice frameworks**. Early in development, the A360 team created a comprehensive **“Consultation Excellence Playbook” library**, which serves two purposes: (1) to train human users (practices get access to these coaching materials), and (2) to provide a knowledge base for the AI to reference when evaluating consults.

**Consultation Performance Coaching Categories:** The core framework divides a consultation into **five phases** (often cited in sales coaching for aesthetic consults): **1\) Connection & Rapport Building, 2\) Discovery & Assessment, 3\) Education & Credentialing, 4\) Care Planning & Treatment Presentation, 5\) Commitment & Next Steps**. For each phase, A360 has documented the ideal behaviors. For example, in Phase 1 (Rapport), best practices include building trust, using the patient’s name, establishing a comfortable environment. In Phase 2 (Discovery), it’s about asking open-ended questions to fully understand the patient’s concerns and medical history, actively listening, and screening for contraindications. By Phase 4 (Planning), best practices include clearly explaining the recommended treatments, why they’re suggested (linking back to the patient’s goals), checking for understanding, and discussing pricing transparently. Phase 5 (Closing) covers confirming the next steps (booking or follow-up), ensuring the patient’s questions are answered, and summarizing the plan (the snippet shows a safety-specific variant of phases, but the general structure applies to all consults).

Additionally, A360 created **thematic coaching categories** that cut across these phases:

* **Objection Handling and Barrier Mastery** – techniques for properly addressing patient concerns (never ignoring or dismissing them). The LAER framework (Listen, Acknowledge, Explore, Respond) is a cornerstone here, as we saw. Playbooks in this category give examples of good vs bad responses to common objections (price, pain, downtime, spousal approval) and emphasize **“Always acknowledge and address every objection”**. They provide model language and encourage confirming that the concern is resolved.

* **Closing and Conversion Precision** – focusing on the end of the consult, ensuring the provider actually asks for the commitment in a professional way, and if the patient is hesitant, exploring why. High performers tend to explicitly ask “Would you like to schedule this treatment?” whereas average performers might just say “think about it.” This playbook draws those contrasts.

* **Education and Trust Building** – guidelines on how to educate the patient (e.g., use simple analogies, don’t overwhelm with jargon, position yourself as a knowledgeable partner). High trust consults lead to higher conversion.

* **Financial Discussion and Value Framing** – best practices for talking about price (don’t be apologetic about cost; frame treatments as investments in outcomes; present financing options if available). A360’s playbook suggests, for instance, always discussing pricing in terms of value and over a relevant time horizon (“this filler lasts 1 year, so think of it as $X per month of looking refreshed”) rather than just the lump sum, to improve acceptance.

* **Follow-up and Re-engagement** – covering what happens after the consult if the patient doesn’t book. This includes setting the expectation for follow-up during the consult (“If you’re not ready today, no worries – I’ll have my coordinator reach out next week to see if you have any questions”). It also covers reactivating past patients by referencing their previous consult data (which A360 Reach automates).

* **Emotional Intelligence & Empathy** – coaching providers to be empathetic, read patient’s emotional cues, and respond appropriately (for example, recognizing if a patient is anxious and slowing down to reassure them, etc.).

These playbooks are quite detailed. They often compare **“average” vs “high-performance” scenarios**. For instance, a snippet in one playbook compares how an average provider might handle a price objection versus how a top 1% provider handles it. Average: gloss over or push scheduling; High-performer: acknowledge (“I understand cost is a concern”), then break down value (“this lasts 4 months, so it’s about $X/month, plus we have financing if you’d like”) and **offer a solution**, then confirm the patient is comfortable. These differences are encoded as tips that the AI uses for evaluation.

The A360 system uses these playbooks both **in real-time prompting of the LLM** (including key points in the system message so the AI knows what “good” looks like), and in generating the **coaching output** text. So a coaching report for a consult might say: *“Provider did not explore the patient’s hesitation about downtime. Best Practice: Use LAER – e.g., ‘I understand you’re worried about downtime (Acknowledge). May I ask what part of downtime concerns you most? (Explore)…’”* thereby directly referencing the taught framework.

**Upsell/Cross-Sell Strategies:** A significant part of maximizing revenue in aesthetic practices is skillfully upselling or cross-selling additional services **ethically** and effectively. A360’s playbooks dedicate an entire category to **“Upsell \+ Cross-Sell \+ Packaging”** strategies. These guide providers on how to introduce complementary treatments or products in a way that feels natural and in the patient’s interest (not pushy). Some highlights from these playbooks:

* **Bundle Recommendations**: Instead of selling one treatment at a time, high performers create bundles (e.g. “Complete Rejuvenation Package” combining Botox \+ Skin Tightening \+ skincare products). The playbook “How to Bundle Botox \+ Skin Tightening” explains how to frame a combination as *the best solution for the patient’s goals* (and incidentally a higher-value sale). Key principles include *presenting bundles as recommendations, not as an upsell*, focusing on the synergistic benefit (“doing these together gives a better result and saves you time”), and transparently discussing cost as an investment in a better outcome.

* **Tiered Options (Good/Better/Best)**: Another playbook advises offering options at different investment levels, so the patient can choose. For example, “good” might be a basic treatment, “better” adds an extra service, “best” is a full makeover. The idea is to show what’s possible at higher levels without pressuring – many patients will self-select somewhere in the middle or top if they see the value. Providers are coached to introduce this by saying something like, “There are a few approaches we can take – one is very straightforward, another is more comprehensive. Let’s discuss which fits you best,” rather than assumptively pushing the highest tier.

* **Long-term Planning**: There’s emphasis on converting a one-time consult into a long-term relationship. Playbooks encourage **Annual Planning Conversations** – discussing not just today’s treatment but what the patient might consider over the next year or two (maintenance treatments, future goals). The concept of lifetime value comes in: *expanding the scope* from a single service to a roadmap of care can 3-4x a patient’s lifetime value to the practice.

* **Ethical Upsell**: All these strategies stress ethics – recommending only what’s suitable, never pushing for revenue at the expense of trust. One guideline that appears in the playbooks is: *“Frame it as a professional recommendation, not as selling. Focus on results, not revenue. Be transparent about costs.”*. Those three points succinctly summarize the tone A360 wants providers to take. The system’s coaching will call out if a provider did something like heavy-handed upselling (e.g., “Provider pushed additional services that were not aligned to stated patient goals – this can undermine trust.”).

**Transcript Evaluation Standards (LAER, KPI Scoring):** We’ve touched on LAER for objection handling; it’s a clear standard the system looks for – did the provider follow those steps when an objection arose? The AI can detect, for instance, if the provider immediately responded with reassurance (a good sign) versus ignoring the objection (bad). The LAER acronym is explicitly listed in the coaching recommendations as a checklist, and A360 even suggests in action items to *“Train all staff on proper objection handling (LAER framework)”* for a practice that was underperforming there.

On the KPI side, A360 has defined around 20-30 KPIs (numbered in some docs). These include ones like “Consultation ended without summary” (as discussed), “% of objections resolved”, “Plan clarity score”, “Time spent discussing goals”, “Follow-up scheduled or not”, etc. Each KPI has a formula and often an associated benchmark range (industry average, high performer, etc.). The scoring for some KPIs is binary (did something happen or not), for others it’s percentage-based or composite. A360’s coaching dashboard uses these to give practices an objective scoring. For example, a practice might get an overall “Consultation Performance Score” derived from multiple KPIs. In one example excerpt, the practice had **1.3% of consults without summary (Excellent)** and **64% unresolved objections (Critical)** – clearly showing a strength in closing consultations properly, but a weakness in handling objections. The platform guides the practice to focus on the weak areas with targeted training (in this case, objection handling). Over time, as the practice implements the coaching tips, they should see those KPI percentages improve – and they have the system to verify it.

Finally, A360 continuously refines these playbooks and standards. They treat the playbooks as a living knowledge base. The Project Overview doc suggests referencing multiple documents in the prompts to the AI. For opportunities extraction, they have playbooks on specific services (e.g., injectables, lasers) to help the AI recognize nuances or suggest typical upsells. For coaching, they reference the core best practices and also **“Average vs High Performer Comparison”** docs to calibrate the AI’s evaluation of a given transcript. This ensures the AI’s feedback isn’t arbitrary – it’s grounded in an established performance framework that resonates with what practice managers already know as best practices.

In summary, A360’s playbooks and best practices are the knowledge foundation that makes its AI outputs credible and valuable. By codifying what top-performing aesthetic consults look like (and conversely, common pitfalls), A360 can not only measure consultations but also provide *actionable guidance* to elevate each consult to that gold standard. It’s like having a coach and consultant built into the software, using decades of industry wisdom (like LAER, consult sales techniques) delivered at scale through AI. Practices using A360 aren’t just getting raw data about their consults – they’re getting a continuous education in how to be better, backed by both human expertise and AI analysis.

## **6\. Outstanding Gaps and Future Plans**

While A360 has built an extensive platform in its beta phase, there are still **some gaps, limitations, and planned enhancements** on the roadmap:

* **Opportunity Tracking V2**: The current opportunity extraction (V1) identifies and logs opportunities, but the next step is to turn that into a robust, configurable **Opportunity Management system**. In V2, A360 plans to introduce **configurable scoring parameters** – practices will be able to adjust probability weights or value multipliers for different opportunity types based on their experience. For example, a mention of interest with no booking might by default be 50% likely to convert, but a particular practice might know their rate is 30%, so they could tune that. They’ll also allow customization of what counts as a “hot” lead vs “warm” (stage thresholds), and how quickly an opportunity decays if not followed up (time decay). The **UI will likely gain a settings panel** to manage these and a more detailed opportunity dashboard with filtering by stage and probability. Additionally, integration with CRM (like GHL) will be further tightened – ensuring two-way sync so that if a deal is closed in GHL it updates A360, etc. The **Opportunity Tracking Requirements doc** hints at more advanced calculations and user-facing explainers for each metric (so staff know how the system is valuing opportunities). In short, the future state is a mature CRM-light system tailored to consultative sales, eliminating the need for manual tracking spreadsheets or separate CRM work for many practices.

* **Product/Service Library Migration & Integration**: Currently, A360’s AI relies on a “practice library” of services and products for grounding recommendations (e.g., mapping “botox” to a service code and knowing its typical aftercare). In the beta, this was implemented as a temporary or static library file. One future improvement is to fully integrate this with the practice’s actual database of services – either by syncing from the practice’s EMR or by managing it within A360. The **migration** involves moving from the static RAG setup to a dynamic, maintained catalog likely stored in Postgres. This will reduce any discrepancies (e.g., if a practice adds a new laser service, the AI will immediately know about it) and allow the AI to only recommend things the practice actually offers. It also opens up the possibility for A360 to support **multi-location practices** where each location might have a slightly different service menu – something a static list can’t easily handle but a database-driven approach can (with practice\_id or location\_id scopes). The plan doc explicitly notes that developers must **“validate all proposed fields and schema against the existing A360 database structure”** for TCP, which suggests aligning the plan generation with the real database of services/products. This implies active work to reconcile any differences (field names, IDs) and likely to build an interface for practices to update their catalog in A360 if it’s not auto-synced.

* **Deeper EHR/CRM Integrations (CRM Sync)**: A360 has started integrating with systems like Zenoti (for scheduling/EMR) and GoHighLevel (for CRM/marketing). The **near-term plan** is to integrate via a partner like Kareo/Karegon to access a wide range of EMRs through a single API connection. This will allow, for example, pulling patient demographics or pushing A360’s notes into the official medical record automatically. It will also enable writing back things like booked treatments into A360’s data (closing the loop on conversion tracking). **Two-way sync** is an important future capability: currently, A360 might create an opportunity in GHL, but if that opportunity is marked as won or lost in GHL by the front desk, A360 should update its internal status too. The code snippet from the GHLSyncService shows steps for syncing one-directionally (A360 → GHL) and hints at ensuring idempotency and caching to avoid redundant syncs. In future, similar services might be created for other CRMs or for inbound sync (GHL → A360). Essentially, A360 aims to **seamlessly slot into the practice’s existing tech stack** – so a gap now is that some integrations are one-way or manual. Soon, those will be fully automated and real-time.

* **Not a Full EMR (but expanding)**: A360 explicitly does not intend to replace core EMR functions like scheduling or billing – at least not yet. However, the exec brief notes that over time A360 may add **“native scheduling and payments”** to evolve into more of a practice management suite. This is more of a strategic future plan. Current limitation: practices still need their EMR for appointment scheduling, inventory, etc. In the future, if A360 sees sufficient demand or an advantage, they might incorporate a scheduling module (which could tie into the consult workflow, e.g., automatically schedule follow-up treatments recommended by the plan). So one could imagine Phase 2 or 3 where A360 becomes an all-in-one for non-insurance practices, reducing reliance on legacy EMRs.

* **AI Model Improvements and Real-time Assist**: On the AI side, A360 will continue to refine the models and prompts. One limitation of AI is always accuracy and consistency. They mitigated hallucinations with strict rules, but as they expand to extract more nuanced fields (like very specific aesthetic goals or subtle emotional cues), they may need to incorporate more advanced NLP or even multi-modal input (e.g., analyzing tone from the audio). The roadmap likely includes switching to more powerful models (e.g., GPT-4 or fine-tuned domain models) as needed, always balancing cost. Also, the **real-time assist** features (like live tagging of objections) were kept minimal in V1, but in the future A360 could provide more **live guidance** during the consult – e.g., a live dashboard showing “patient seems hesitant about pricing” to cue the provider. That will require ultra-reliable real-time processing, which is a future frontier (the docs recommended focusing on post-consult first, which they did, and perhaps revisit real-time later).

* **UI Enhancements**: From a UI/UX perspective, planned improvements include: more sophisticated filtering and sorting in dashboards, more card types (as mentioned, possibly an objections card, or splitting products vs services into separate cards), the ability for only one card to expand at a time for cleanliness, etc. The V1 card system intentionally did **no filtering by consult type**, but in the future, once the consult type detection is rock-solid, the UI might tailor what it shows for relevancy (for example, if it was a “Pricing-only” consult, maybe the dashboard highlights pricing discussion details and hides irrelevant fields like treatment plan – currently it doesn’t hide anything, which can lead to blank or N/A sections). Future versions might conditionally show/hide or label fields more clearly by context.

* **Imaging and Visualization**: The exec brief mentioned an **Imaging module** by Q2, which is outside the consult transcript scope but complementary. This suggests a future addition where A360 can incorporate patient photos and imaging analysis (perhaps simulating results or tracking before/after). That would strengthen the platform’s value prop (for example, tying consult recommendations to actual images, or using computer vision to analyze patient features as part of the consult). It’s a gap now (they likely integrate with something like RxPhoto for images, given the founder’s background), but one they plan to fill so the consult conversation and imaging live together.

* **Scalability and Fine-tuning**: As usage grows, one practical challenge is cost and speed of running large models for every consult. The team will be exploring optimizations like fine-tuning smaller models or using embeddings to retrieve relevant info faster. They already talked about possibly fine-tuning the real-time tagger model on their domain data. So, a behind-the-scenes plan is to continuously improve performance such that they can handle thousands of consults per day with low latency and cost. This might not surface to users except as generally faster responses.

* **Pending Items from Beta Feedback**: Some specific feature gaps identified during beta: **Opportunity status tracking over time** (e.g., if a patient didn’t book immediately, tracking whether they eventually converted weeks or months later – currently that linkage might not be fully automated). **Provider adoption and training** – A360 provides the coaching output, but ensuring providers actually use it is more of a change management thing; future versions might include gamification or acknowledgment features (like a way for providers to review their own KPIs and compare with peers in-app). Also, **multi-provider consults** (if two providers see a patient together) and other edge cases might not be fully handled yet.

In conclusion, A360’s future development is focused on **refinement and expansion**: refining core features like opportunity tracking, product catalog integration, and consult-type specificity; and expanding into adjacent areas like deeper integration with existing systems and potentially adding new modules (imaging, scheduling) that complement the consult intelligence. The current system already proves the concept – turning conversations to data to actions – and the planned enhancements aim to make it even more powerful, flexible, and seamlessly integrated into the day-to-day workflow of aesthetic practices. As the executive summary notes, the long-term vision is for A360 to be **“the AI layer the industry runs on”**, whether as a standalone platform or embedded within other software. The near-term roadmap of closing gaps is all about ensuring that A360 can scale reliably and deliver on that vision across a variety of clinical environments, without leaving any piece of the puzzle (data or workflow) behind.
